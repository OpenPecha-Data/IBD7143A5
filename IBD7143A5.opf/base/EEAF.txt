还赠送了一套藏纸制造工具。
当时,西藏制造藏纸的各个地区都认为藏纸制造业的条件以及工
艺非常落后,藏纸制造业无任何前途,因此都想放弃这一行当。此时
强巴尊珠先生了解到藏纸 临失传,到藏纸故乡尼木、达布、朗县
孜龙等地,给生产藏纸的各戶一千元进行扶持,并给他们讲述藏纸工
艺的价值和藏纸发展的前途,以及制作藏纸的重要性。这样藏纸制作
工艺才幸免失传。强巴尊珠先生对藏族传统工艺,尤其是藏纸制造工
艺的抢救和保护方面做出了不可磨灭的贡献。

藏纸制造工艺何时产生各说纷纭。有些学者把《旧唐书》中记载

因为上述案教史书《金光珍宝》中的记载和《东噶藏学辞典》中记
载,文成公主进藏之前,松赞干布派遣大臣迎娶文成公主时,让他的
使者们带去了自己的蓝褐藏纸书写的迎娶信,这说明当时西藏已经有
藏纸制造工艺技术。
另外,《浅谈藏产生和其特点》一文中,也谈到公元前蕃第
二代赞布穆赤赞布时期,从像雄把辛南卡囊瓦多坚为主的128位学者激
请到吐蕃,建立了四十五所学术中心,并翻译了显密等大量的苯教经
典。因此,当时已经就有藏纸生产工艺。如果没有能够书写经文的纸
张就无法实现如此繁多的苯教经文的翻译和书写。
如果,断定松赞干布之前西藏没有造纸技术,那么藏王为什么会
做蓝褐藏纸。蓝褐藏纸的制造技术来源于普通纸的技术,并且其工艺
流程要比制造普通纸的工艺复杂得多。
汉地的造纸技术与藏纸制造的生产从技术上、原料上、产量上、



用途上都有着很大的差异。在诸多历史文献中,仅依据《旧唐书》中

藏纸的主要成分
藏纸的所有材料都是来自于青藏高原上生长的植物,主要的原料
有:1、白芷叶(另名称“给大新19);2、沈皮香;3、瑞香狼毒;
4、萨玛;5、鎮瓦树;6、大麻;7、“托”草的草根等上述材
料也有优劣之分。白芷叶是造纸的最佳材料,这种材料主要生长在森
林、柏树丛中。峡谷、无岩石之地为最佳。生长在沙地、红土地片岩
石等地的较坚硬难以煮熟,且色泛黄,此材料为中等品,而皮厚虫蛀
者为次品。白皮为造纸的上品材料,但作厚纸经卷应选此原料,而
制纸的下品原料应属瑞香狼毒,因有毒而不易虫蛀,能保存长久,还
有其他原料,这些原料是上述材料是主要原料的辅助或补充材料。

藏线的新产品走向现代市场
强巴尊珠先生为首的彩泉民族手工艺研发中心上下通过不懈努
力,邀请有着丰富藏纸制造经验的老工艺师和彩泉福利学校的孤儿一
起进行尝试性的制作,在这一过程中人力、财力、物力都有相当大的
消耗,为了使这一民族工艺不被失传,全体人员再三努力下不断向市
出了藏纸的新工产品达100多种。现在本厂除了蓝褐纸外,还能生
产各种档次的藏纸,按需求可以生产不同薄厚、宽窄各异的用纸,同
样对蓝褐纸的制作工艺研究将会继续下去。
过去藏纸都是按地名命名的,如晋东纸、木纸、堆纸、不丹
纸、康纸、尼木纸等都可以亲眼所见,而很多藏纸由于从前要缴纳公
关公税,所以很多人为了逃避差役而放弃此行当,从事造纸的人越

原材料浸泡时不能有点污渍,甚至不能有一根毛发,尤其在制作藏币
纸方面有特殊讲究。

藏纸的传统产品
1、藏纸与藏文馆藏
众所周知,雪域高原的藏民族自古以来是一个善于收藏各种文化
的民族。包括宗教、医学、天文历算、诗歌、文法等十明学的大量书
籍,以及寺规、公文等卷中央浩繁的文献都是世世代代搜集整理,聚
沙成塔集日夜成装的,特别是在佛像佛经佛塔三个皈依中,又把佛经
作为精神支柱三宝中的一宝,除供奉在藏区的众多寺庙和大多数老
百姓家中之外,还安装藏在无数的佛塔里供人们瞻仰。这些幸存下来
的古籍无论是数量还是质量都属世界罕见的瑰宝,可以和任何民族的
瑰宝相媲美,而这些古籍尽管书法和版本不同,但所用的纸都是藏区
本地产的纸,从这些留存下来的古籍中不难看出,过去藏纸的产量之
多,用途之广是其它物品有难以比拟。
2、藏纸与纸币

的各种币、钞,无论从纸质还是图案设计都凝聚了藏民族的智慧和灿
烂文化的精髓。1792年,西藏地方政府在驻藏大臣监督下,在工布地
区党木宗造出一种“章嘎”,由于1792年是藏历十三饶河的第四十六
年,所以纸币面中央铸有藏文“
久松西珠”(汉文译为十三,四六)

章卡计算,其名称为“章嘎”即五个章嘎,作藏银一两五钱;“章
久哦”即十五个章嘎,作藏银二两二钱五分;“章卡尼秀则残”即

Tibetan Paper
Protecting and developing the Technology of
Traditional Tibetan Papermaking
Tibet, one of the 56 nationalities in the People's Republic of China, has a
unique character and a long history. Our Tibetan ancestors struggled with harsh
environmental conditions and a shortage of oxygen while adapting from generation
to generation to a life on the highest Plateau in the world. As a result, with their own
wisdom and determination, they have created the material and spiritual civilization
which constitutes Tibetan culture. Therefore they have left a great cultural heritage
with a unique Tibetan character for the next generations. However, in recent times,
some parts of this cultural heritage have almost disappeared and, in particular, some
valuable traditional manufacturing skills were nearly lost. At the time that the Third
Plenary Session of the 11th Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party
was held, the Chinese Communist Party Central Committee and the State Council
repeatedly stressed that it is a crucial responsibility of our generation to preserve,
inherit and develop the great ancient cultural heritage of every nationality of China.
Responding to this call from the Central Committee of the CCP, Jatson Research
and Development Center for Ethnic Handicrafts in the Chenguan District of the
Lhasa Municipality has taken the initiative to preserve some traditional Tibetan
handicraft skills. In particular, it has focused on the preservation of Tibetan
papermaking skill and it has started producing Tibetan paper. At the same time
progress has been made in research work on Tibetan paper.
In order to protect this skill, Jampa Tsundhup, the director of the Jatson
Chumig Handicraft Workshop, together with his colleagues Mr. Lochoe and Mr.
Ngagwang Choyang have visited Dagbo Gyatsa, Zelung in Nang County, Nyemo,
and Ngamring in Western Central Tibet. Tenzin Choedon, daughter of the
director, was sent to observe and study Tibetan papermaking technical skills in the
Dege area. We have mainly studied the characteristic of papermaking skills and its
material resources in the above places. Also we have invited the following sixteen
experts in papermaking from different places: Tashi, Mobo Kalsang, Takog, Pema



Chodron, Tsomo from Nang County, Popo Gokgok, (76 years old - one of the best
known experts in papermaking in the Nyimo area), Dum Tewang from Western
Central Tibet, Norgyal, a Tibetan calligrapher from Nyimo, Sopal from Lhasa,
Professor Tashi Tsering from Tibet University in Lhasa, some members of “Paper
Road to Tibet” (shog bu’i lam) in America including Jane Farmer, Tom Leech, Jim
Canary and Carol Brighton. We have also invited two professional paper producers
from Nepal: Mr. Nyima Dawa and Thupdan. We clearly understand that paper
is a very important commodity in our society and that Tibetan paper has unique
characteristics, different from other papers. We have held meetings and discussed
the following topics: the development of papermaking technological skills in
the world; the importance of spreading and developing papermaking skills; the
ways to study and develop Tibetan papermaking skills and materials; the ways to
exchange papermaking technical skills between China and foreign countries and
the ways to pass on and develop Tibetan papermaking skills in future and for future
generations.
The director Jampa Tsundhup thought that Tibetan papermaking skills could
not be preserved unless some young people were trained. Therefore, he selected
five students: Chojor, Thupdan Tsering, Tsering Dorje, Migmar (a girl) and Yudon
(a girl) with a basic literacy in Tibetan from the Jatson Chumig Welfare Special
School and trained them in Tibetan papermaking skills. In the second round, he
trained nine students in papermaking skills: Tashi Topyal, Chogo, Nyima Dhundup,
Sonam Yeshi, Jampel, Nyidron, Migmar, Tsamdrul and Yangzom. In the third
round, he trained five students: Pasang Chungda, Rizin Chogyal, Sonam Dhundup,
Jamdor and Gakyi. In addition, he has received a request from two students at
Ronbok Monastery in Western Central Tibet. During their stay, the groups were
paid their tuition and living expenses and also provided them with some sets of
papermaking equipment.
At that time Tibetan paper makers from the areas where traditional papermaking
skills were practiced were thinking: first; there was no market for Tibetan
papers. Secondly, Tibetan papermaking skills won't have a future as nobody was
interested in them. Lastly, no money is being put into the manufacture of Tibetan
papermaking. As a result, the people with Tibetan papermaking skills were
discouraged and the industry of manufacturing Tibetan paper had been inactive for
about a decade.



However, as mentioned above, Jampa Tsundhup, Jatson Chumig Welfare
Handicraft Workshop, realized that there was a serious risk of losing traditional
Tibetan papermaking skills. He went to the original papermaking places such as
Nyimo, Dagpo, and Zelung in Nang County. He gave one thousand Ren Min Bi
(Yuan) to each family of papermaking experts and encouraged them to continue
producing Tibetan paper by telling them the great value of Tibetan papermaking
skills and its bright future. He convinced the papermakers and they became very
motivated as they had a good understanding of the reason to continue to produce
Tibetan paper. The skills of Tibetan papermaking were thus rescued from its
decline and the manufacturing of Tibetan paper was started again. Thanks to the
constant efforts made by Jampa Tsundhup in preserving and developing Tibetan
traditional handicraft skills, Tibetan papermaking was restored and improved.
The History of Tibetan Papermaking
According to historical records, documents were ordinarily written on bark, slab
stone, sheep bone, wood block, bamboo and sheepskin. This was proved to be the
case by conducting archeological studies. Bon was the indigenous religion of Tibet
in ancient times and one of the teachings of Shenrab Mibo, the Buddha of Bon is
known as (Gser ‘od nor bu me ‘bar)) (the flaming jewels with golden light). This
Bon text refers to Tibetan paper by stating that:
"If one reads, with respect and politeness, the texts that were written in gold on
blue paper (mthing shog sngon mo) that has the colour of turquoise, this can drive
away all obstacles and negative forces and will make one to be born as the universal
monarch of a billion-fold world system and ultimately achieve full enlightenment.
If one reads, with respect and politeness, the texts that were written in silver on
blue paper (mthing shog sngon mo) that has the colour of turquoise, this can drive
away all obstacles and negative forces and will make one to be born as the universal
monarch of a billion-fold world system and ultimately achieve full enlightenment.
If one reads, with respect and politeness, the texts that were written in turquoise
ink on red paper (mtshal shog dmar po) that has the colour of coral, this can



drive away all obstacles and negative forces and will make one to be born as the
universal monarch of a billion-fold world system and ultimately achieve perfect full
enlightenment.
If one reads, with respect and politeness, the texts that were written in conch
shell and bronze ink on blue paper (mthing shong sngon po) that has the colour of
turquoise, this can drive away all obstacles and negative forces and will make one
to be born as the universal monarch of a billion-fold world system and ultimately
achieve full enlightenment.

This text clearly shows that Tibetan papermaking techniques had been used at

In addition in The Dungkar Encyclopedic Dictionary it is stated that Tibetan
papermaking skills were developed during the reign of the Thirty-seventh King
Thrisong Detsen so that volumes of texts and manuscripts could be produced. At
the time, it is claimed that the people who were preaching Buddhism, including
masters, abbots, thirteen teachers, twenty five students, twenty five mediators were
given between two to four volumes of paper sheets every month according to their
status or ranking. It is said that, at the time, important statements made by the King
and official financial documents had to be recorded on Tibetan paper and stored in
archives.
Moreover, during this time when the red part of the Potala Palace was being
built, about eight different kinds of papers made by Tibetans were used. According
to the table of contents of the 'Gser gdung 'dzam gling rgyan gcig' these papers
were produced in different parts of Tibet using local materials and these papers
were produced according to traditional Tibetan papermaking techniques.



Geshe Deumar Tendzin Phuntsog, a well known Tibetan scholar, gives a
detailed account of the ways of making different kinds of Tibetan paper and the arts
of papermaking techniques in his book “Mirror of Handicrafts" (bzo yig pa tra).
If one analyses both the written historical accounts given by Tibetan scholars
and oral claims made by ordinary people, it is clear that Tibetan papermaking has a
longer history than has been claimed.
It is a well known historical fact that the King Songtsan Gampo wrote letters on
Tibetan blue paper (mthing shog) in golden and silver ink to the King of Nepal and
the Emperor of China asking to marry their princesses respectively. Furthermore,
just judging the use of the term “mthing shog" (blue paper) itself, we can clearly
see that papermaking techniques were spread widely in Tibet a long time ago and
this was confirmed by senior Tibetan scholars.
As to the question of when Tibetan papermaking skill originated in Tibet, some
people claim that papermaking skills did not exist in Tibet prior to the arrival of the
Chinese princess Wenchen. They do this by quoting a few lines from the Old Book
of the Tang Dynasty: "... it was agreed to send (to Tibet] people with skills to make
wine, water-mills, paper, ink ...". This claim is unacceptable. If one studies the
quotes used earlier from the classical Bon scripture (Gser od nor bu me ‘bar))
(the flaming jewels with golden light), and analyses the reference made to Tibetan
papermaking skills in Tibet in the Dungkar Encyclopedic Dictionary, it is clear
that papermaking techniques already existed in Tibet prior to princess Wenchen's
arrival. In fact the Dungkar Encyclopedic Dictionary states that “During the reign
of Songtsen Gampo, a letter was written in Chinese language to the Chinese
Emperor Tang Taizong asking to marry Princess Wenchen".
In addition, the article called A brief introduction to the origin of traditional
Tibetan paper and its features claims, by referring to Bon scriptures, that around
the beginning of the Western era “Mutri Tsanpo, the Second Tibetan King, invited
one hundred and eight great scholars including Shen Namkha Nangwa Dogchen
from Shangshung to Tibet and established 45 large Bon religious gatherings and
translated Bon scriptures such as sutra, tantric treatises and so on, producing a great
number of volumes."

Traditional Tibetan Papers Produced
From Different Parts of the Region:
Their Features and Usages
The following is a brief introduction to the features of traditional Tibetan paper
production.
The most famous paper is Kyemshog from the Kyemdong area, at border
between Kongpo and Dagpo. The best quality of “Kyemshog” was used especially
for printing Tibetan paper currency. There are “Kyemshog” papers with different
sizes, such as “three arm spans (Dom Summa)”, “two arm spans (Dom Nyima)”,
“one arm span (Dom Gangma)" and even shorter in length (“Dom” is a Tibetan
measuring unit which corresponds to around 1.7 meters). The longer ones were
used for writing or printing official documents, such as “Re Lung Tsatsig” (official
regulations over hunting restriction on special days), “Official Stables Registration"
and "New Year greetings,” etc. The shorter ones were used for documents such as
public notices and verdicts that needed to be stamped.
“Dag shog” is another kind of high quality paper that was used to write other
formal documents including public notices and verdicts that needed to be stamped
with official seals. “Dag shog" was produced in the “Dagpo” area (today's Nyingtri
Prefecture)
Another kind of paper is called “Drug shog” produced in “Drug yul”, which
refers to today's Buthan. This kind of paper was used to print precious religious
scriptures including the Kagyur and Tengyur, monastery administrative records and
property transaction documents, etc.
“Gyang yug or Gyal yug”, a paper produced in Gyaltse area, was used for
formal communication between governmental departments with formal headings/
stamps.
The “Toe shog (stod shog)" used to be produced in the western part of Tibet.
This kind of paper was used to write on lengthy official documents such as
announcements and regulations. Another kind of paper from western Tibet was
announcen
was



called “Lo shog” and was produced in the Lo area (Mustang). This was used for the
same purposes as the Toe shog.
There were two kinds of “Tsang shog” which used to be produced in the
Shigatse area. One kind of “Tsang shog", which has a slightly redder color and
smoother surface, was used by Tashilhumpo Monastery and the other one with a
rough surface was used by Sakya Monastery.
The kind of paper called “Tsona Leyshok” is obviously produced in the Tsona
area in southern Tibet. This is a kind of paper that was produced for the purpose of
official uses. “Tsona Leyshok” was used by different government departments to
write short memos and to make notes.
“Kongshok or Shoshok”, produced in Kongpo area, was used for writing general
documents.
“Nyeshok”, produced in Nyemo area, wasn't widely used for writing important
official documents as it absorbs ink too easily.
The use of the different kinds of Tibetan paper listed above varies according to
the quality of paper that was produced in the different areas. Of course, there were
other kinds of locally produced paper from other parts of Tibet, but here I have
only mentioned the well known ones or the ones widely mentioned in accounts on
Tibetan paper. These are just some of the most popular local forms of Tibetan paper
used prior to the peaceful liberation.
Tibetan Paper and Tibetan document
It is a well-known fact that the tradition of preserving documents from different
times has a long history in Tibet. The historical documents preserved from
generation to generation cover various fields, such as religious texts, traditional
medicine, astronomy and astrology, rhetoric, monastic records and many other
official documents and so forth. Especially, as Buddhist scriptures have been
regarded as the 'speech', i.e. one of the three aspects (the body, the speech and
the mind) of the Lord Buddha. They are kept in a great number of monasteries,



pagodas and individual homes throughout the country. It is certainly a great honor
for the people living on the Tibet Plateau that they have such a large amount of high
quality scriptures with such important contents.
All the documents preserved before the 1950s were written and printed on
traditional Tibetan paper. These documents were either printed or written with
the two common styles of calligraphy, the "Uchen"(the headed letter) and the
"Umey"(the headless letter). The scriptures that are currently being kept in
libraries and monasteries fall into two categories: “Pering” (the long sheet one) and
“Pethung”(the short sheet one).
As mentioned previously, compared to other cultural relics, the scriptures
preserved in thousands of monasteries throughout Tibet are much more numerous.
From this we can see the wide and greater use of Tibetan papers in the past.
Traditional Tibetan Paper and the Kites
Flying kites is one of the traditional entertaining activities in Tibet; it is
especially popular in central Tibet. The kite is called "Chapeer (flying bird)" in the
Lhasa area and "Shogcha (paper bird)" in the Shigatse area.
The kites that people fly in Tibet are made of traditional Tibetan paper and
bamboo. A piece of rectangular ( almost square - the distance of top to bottom
is slightly shorter than right to left) shaped paper is supported by two pieces of
bamboo; one piece of bamboo (the upper 2/3 part is thicker than the last 1/3 part)
is stuck vertically from the top to bottom, and the other bamboo is stuck arched
across over the first bamboo from left to right so that the paper is stretched to its
maximum. Sometimes, a long, thin paper is fixed to the bottom as the tail of the
kite.
There has been an informal, but very strict, rule which everyone has to follow
regarding when kites can be flown. Only when the harvest is finished and farmers
have started processing their crops outdoors can one fly a kite. Then you will see
kites with different drawings flying in the clear, blue sky.
Besides a white, plain kite, there are kites with various colorful drawings, such



as "Gya Wu (the bearded)", "Mig Log (the wrathful eyes)", "Che Wa (the wrathful
teeth)" and "Bangthen (the apron)", etc. These drawings represent power and
maturity, arrogance, challenge and beauty, respectively. One of the differences
between the traditions of kites flying in Tibet and inland China is the involvement
of fighting. In Tibet people enjoy fighting each others' kites high in the sky, which
you cannot find in the traditional Chinese way of flying kites.
Prior to the peaceful liberation, there were a few Muslim families in Lhasa
selling kites, and the two most famous families were "Cha" and "Chaje Tsa".
For a long time in Tibet flying kites has been a popular leisure activity not only
among the poorer people but many aristocrats and high religious figures were also
fond of it. It is said that both the regent Rating Lama and Pagpala Geleg Namgyal
liked flying kites very much. The tradition of flying kites in Tibet not only includes
the visual and performing arts, with the fighting part of the game, it also brings
people excitement and funs. This also makes it different from kites that are flown in
other parts of China.
Kites that are made of traditional Tibetan paper seem to be tougher and easier to
fly high.
Tibetan Paper and Tibetan Postage Stamps
Tibetan people have dominated the Tibetan Plateau for a long period of time and
have created the famous Tibetan culture using their wisdom and abilities. This has
brightened the dignity of the Chinese national cultural heritage.
Postage is a way of communicating between human beings, and was developed
in ancient times. Postage systems have been developed over time according to
social structures and the frequency of communication among human beings.
According to historical texts, there were more than five or six post offices during
the Tibetan imperial period. At that time more than ten thousands kilometers
separated post offices.
From the perspective of the development of Tibetan postage the time when the
regional Tibetan government established post offices was crucial and a special
moment in Tibetan history. The establishment of the institution of the post office



brought to the fore the development of transportation routes, new rules and the
issue of several stamps. The fact that this was a long process demonstrated the
wishes of the Tibetan people for social progress and the development of human
civilization.
According to our investigations, on May 26, 1912, the Tibetan local government
established a post office which was situated at Tengye Ling monastery in Lhasa. An
fourth rank official in the Kashag administration was appointed as the director of
the post office. The Tibetan local post office issued the first set of postage stamps of
which there were 6 types: green, dark blue, reddish brown, dark red, vermillion and
light green.
The postage stamp price ranged from 2.5 cents, to 5 cents, 7.5 cents, 1 sang (a
unit of weight 1 / 10 Liang=5grams), 1.5 sang.
The Tibetan monetary unit can be breaken down into: Khagang, Karma,
Chegye, Shogang, Tamka Chig and Sang Chig in Tibetan. Moreover, the Tibet local
government produced a second version of postage which has two kinds of stamps.
These have different colors and values. The colors are blue and red, and the values
are four Tamka and eight Tamka respectfully.
In 1933 the Tibetan local government published five sets of postage stamps.
These stamps have different colors and various values. There were seven different
stamps, including: Chegya which was yellow, Shogang which was blue, one Tamka
which was orange, two Tamka was red, and four Tamka was green. The Tibetan
local government issued the first and third set of stamps which were produced
using Tibetan paper. The stamps were produced by hand printing techniques and
machines, but without water colors and glues. However, the third set of general
stamps were produced using a sewing machine, and the stamps produced had a
jagged shape.
The government document stamp is a kind of postage which is published in
many countries. The function of these stamps was evidence that could not only
prove a free payment for sending government documents, but could also be used
as evidence about post payment. Tibet document stamps were published at a later
stage in a variety of forms with higher values and a bigger shape. Compared with



mainland China and foreign countries stamps, they have unique characteristics and
features. The Tibetan local government published a variety and number of stamps
but there is no detailed information about them. According to The Post Office
Bureau of the Tibet Autonomous Region, the officers have found eight different
stamps.
The Telecom stamp is a kind of paid stamp for sending telecom. Except in
Tibet, we could not find a telecom stamp used in mainland China. According to
investigation, in 1950, the Tibetan local government issued telecom stamps which
had been printed on a handmade copper printer. Firstly, the printer was hung on the
wood block, and then Tibetan paper hand pressed on to it. The stamp was without
polished glue.
In May 26 1912 the Tibetan local government established Drakhang (the post
office). Thus the Drangkhang has almost a 47 year history. Tibetan local stamps
were not only a piece of history but also provided a use for Tibetan paper. They
provide a unique, characteristic and vivid set of cultural information.
Tibetan Paper and Tibetan Currency
In 1792, the Tibetan local government printed the first Tibetan currency called
Chusum Shedrug (i.e. thirteen forty-six in Tibetan), which was used until March
1959. After 1959 the Tibet local currency was abandoned and was no longer
used. Therefore the Tibetan currency has a 167 year old history. The currency in
all its aspects, including the quality of paper and design, represented the wisdom
and vivid culture of Tibet. In 1792, Tibetan currency was first made by the local
Tibetan government, under the supervision of the Amban (a representative of the
Qing court in China), in Jomo Dzong, in the Kongpo area. In the Tibetan calendar,
1792 was the the 46th year of the 13th Rabjung. Therefore, 1346 was printed in the
middle of the paper currency produced in that year.
In 1912 the Tibetan local government established a banking system and the
Drabchi Mint opened again. At the same time the Tibetan local government used
wood blocks to manually print five kinds of paper currency.
The value of currency was designated using the Tamka unit. Five Tamka is
equal to one Sang and five Sho of Tibetan silver. Fifteen Tamka is two Sang and



five Sho of Tibetan Silver. Twenty-five Tamka is two Sang, two Sho and five Kar
of Tibetan silver. Fifty Tamka is three Sang, seven Sho and five Kar of Tibetan
silver, it is also called one Doze. If a paper note worth seven Sang and five Sho was
produced, this would be regarded as a forged note. Hand written numbers were
printed at the edge of the paper currency.
In 1918 the Tibetan local government established the Nortoe Sertam Mint at
Nortoe Sertam House in the western part of Nortoe Lingka. From 1920 it started
producing Sertam currency. At the same time, Nortoe Sertam Mint produced copper
coins of one Sho, paper currency of seven Sang and five Sho respectively with a
machine that used water power.
In 1922 the Dogde Mint was established in the Dogde area in North Lhasa. In
1926, a paper currency called fifty Tamka with a complete illustration started to
be produced using a machine. In the period up to 1941, one million and twenty
thousand (1,020,000) paper currency notes were printed the total of which is worth
seven million and six hundred fifty thousand (7,650,000) measures of Tibetan
silver. These Tibetan paper notes were the earliest printed money with a complete
colour illustration. According to research work on the bank that was responsible
for currency distribution the bank was initially known as Tashi Office. During
the fifteen years from 1920, the time when the printing was started, till 1944, nine
hundred and fifty seven thousand and eight hundred and ninety six paper currency
sheets were printed and distributed.
In 1937 the Radreng regent of the local government approved the use of an
iron model printer to print one hundred notes of Tibetan paper money. In 1949 the
Tagdrag regent approved the printing of twenty five notes of paper money with
colour illustrations.
It is worth noting here that the two different kinds of currencies mentioned
above were designed by Lobsang Norbu, who was not more than twenty three years
old at the time but held the title of Uchung, and a professional Thangka artisan of
Shol Dopal. He perfectly combined traditional Tibetan painting with the technique
of producing Tibetan paper currency.
According to research works it is claimed that Tibetan paper currencies



produced between 1912 and 1913 were printed manually using wood blocks and
therefore the colour and illustrations on the notes were standard.
In Tibetan paper money there are five different notes: five note, ten note, fifteen
note, twenty five note and fifty note.
From 1926 to 1959, five different multicolored Tibetan currencies were printed.
The multicolored fifty notes were produced from 1926 to 1949. The hundred
notes started production in 1937. 1959 was the year when the largest amount of
color notes was printed. In 1946 and 1948, five notes and ten notes were printed
respectively.
The one hundred note was produced using the following method: before gluing
two pieces of Tibetan paper sheets together, the script “Gaden Phodrang Chogle
Namgyal / The Tibet Government" was written in Tibetan in the middle of one the
sheets. After this had been done, the two sheets were glued together. This was done
to prevent forgery.
After finishing these steps, scripts and illustrations were printed on both sides
of the paper currency using water printing. Numbers on the Tibetan currency were
written by hand which is unique to Tibetan paper currency.
According to the statistics, Drabchi Mint printed notes to the value of 1,710,926
Tibetan silver Sang and distributed Them. Among these, 71,015 Sang in value
of paper currency was printed between 1935 and 1950. Between 1951 and 1959
a value of 97,192 Sang of paper currency was printed and distributed. In total a
value of 106,905 Sang of Tibetan paper currency was printed and issued.
The development of Tibetan paper currency printing boosted the amount of
Tibetan paper production and it also enlarged the usage of Tibetan paper. This
promoted the development of traditional papermaking techniques, and also
encouraged improvements in the quality of Tibetan paper.



Introduction to the Materials used in Papermaking
All the materials that were used to produce Tibetan paper are plants that grow
on the high Tibetan Plateau. These plants include mainly 1. Kyemshing (a variety
of Daphne), 2. the bark of Agara tree, 3. Stellera, 4. Sama, 5. Shing gropa, 6.
Cannabis, 7. the root of Ton and so forth. These materials can be categorized into
a range of qualities: high, medium and low. First, Kyemshing is the best quality
material to be used in producing paper. Kyemshing grows in the jungles and forests
and in low land valleys and places where rocks are not of high quality. The middle
quality grows in the desert, red soil and rock, which has several characteristics:
it is hard and difficult to boil and its color is also a bit yellowish. The low quality
Kyemshing is thick barked with insects inside.
Generally speaking, Kyemshing is the best quality of the materials for Tibetan
papermaking. The second high quality material is called Agara and this is good for
making thick paper that is ideal for writing a large volume of scriptures. Although
it is claimed that the lowest quality of material is the root of Stellera Chamaejasme,
it contains poison therefore it prevents insects from damaging the paper, which is
thus preserved and lasts a long time. The other materials have the same functions
as those that have been mentioned before. Sometime materials can be replaced by
others when there is a shortage of one particular type of material.
The Ancient Technology of the Tibetan Papermaking
The process of papermaking has several steps: The process described here refers
to the production of paper from daphne or daphne related bushes (for plants in
which roots are used the process is similar). First, peel off the bark; Second, dry
it; Third, shake the bark until all the dust is completely removed; Fourth, boil it
in water; Fifth, beat the bark with a wooden hammer on a flat stone; Sixth, boil
it again; Seventh, add alkali for processing (if you haven't got alkali, carbon can
be used and added in the hot water); this can accelerate the process of producing
the paper pulp. Eighth, pour out the pulp onto a flat, thin cloth for drying. Textured
finishes, watermarks and wire patterns which imitate hand-made paper can be
created by the use of appropriate rollers in the later stages of machine processing.

A paper web is produced from which the water must eventually be removed by

be taken out from the water to dry in the sun. Tenth, in order to make flat paper,

pulp and it is then dried. That is the final step in the paper making process.
In the work of one of the foremost Tibetan papermaking technicians Konchok
Tenzin, bZo gnas skar rtse chu thigs there is an account of the ways to polish paper
with stones. He documents the use of several kinds of polishing stones including
conch shell, stone, jade, iron and bronze, and Zi beads. In the bZo gnas skar rtse
chu thigs it is claimed that using the conch shell to polish will result in the paper
turning blue. If a stone is used to polish, it will make the paper become softer
but the paper shrinks. Using jade to polish will produce a pattern on the paper.
If iron and bronze are used to polish colored spots will appear on the paper. So,
Zi bead is the best polishing stone. This can give the paper a fine color and soft
surface. However, this polishing material is very difficult to get. If a Zi bead is not
available, animal horns, porcelain, and agate are very good materials for making
paper soft and durable. These polishing objects can produce nice paper. According
to these remarks it is not difficult to see how much efforts the older generations had
to put into this process to find many different solutions to produce the best quality
of Tibetan paper.
The Techniques for Making Special Tibetan Paper
The special technique of making blue paper that was used for religious
scriptures with writing on it in golden and silver inks has several processes to cool
off the black vitriol and purifying liquids which prevent earth and dust spoiling the
paper.
Thanu and solder need to be mixed together before any steam comes out,
filtered through a net of fibers. Mix sour wheat wine, solder and black vitriol and
boil them. At first boiling a little solder, black vitriol, sour wheat and Thanu can



be used to test the paper. If the paper turns black, it means the paper is well done.
Then the paper has to be taken out from the pot after it has cooled off for a while.
Powdered Arura is necessary and must be added to the liquid which is then filtered
again with a net of fibers. After that, the processed paper which has been soaked in
the liquid is dried. This is called blue paper. “The Treasures of Knowledge in Snow
Land” reports the techniques for producing blue paper. It describes that the Arura
should be powdered and boiled in the yellow liquid then the paper pulp is taken out
and dried for a moment. After that the paper soaks in the vitriol and the wheat wine
again. If the colour of the paper changes after this process it means the quality of
the blue paper will be excellent. It is claimed that there were many different ways
of producing blue paper.
Features of Traditional Tibetan Paper
Tibetan paper has several features: 1) completely insect free for over a thousand
years 2) It is not necessary to worry that it will be eaten by mice or insects thanks
to the fact that it is poisonous 3) It is finely textured, strong, extremely durable and
completely acid free 4) letters written on Tibetan paper last a long time and will
not fade away 5) Tibetan paper is lighter than other papers, so it is easy to carry or
transport and there is no worrying of overweight when stored on upper floors 6)
soft and strong 7) ink does not sink into the paper 8) extremely durable and shiny
9) does not harm readers' eyes when reading for a long time 10) waste paper can be
reused for making artifacts or other paper.
In 2007, Ngodrub Phuntsok, secretary of the Bureau of Water Conservancy in
Shigatse, visited Japan and he saw that Tibetan paper was used to cover windows
in a few Japanese families that he visited. He asked these Japanese people: “Japan
is a developed industrial country, why do you use Tibetan paper instead of glass?”
They replied by saying this is a choice based on a scientific reason and continued to
state that Tibetan paper has four features; 1) Tibetan paper is hard and extremely
durable 2) can be well ventilated 3) is good at preventing dust 4) keeps away
insects during the summer season. Their reply reminds us that in the past Tibetans
had a tradition of using paper, instead of glass, to cover windows.



All the scholars who did research on the information mentioned above such
as the history of Tibetan paper, raw materials and their resources, the special
characteristics of Tibetan paper produced in different areas, unanimously believe
that Tibetan paper making techniques are a product of the Tibetan peoples' wisdom
and capabilities.
New Products for a Modern Market
Jampa Tsundhup, director of the Jatson Chumig Research and Development
Center for Ethnic Handicrafts in Lhasa in the Chenguan district, has invited several
professional scholars and students who have examined the processes of paper
making. He has put in great efforts, both financially and personally to support
this program and make it possible. He did not report his difficulties to the local
government, and carried all the responsibility for protecting and developing this
priceless traditional technique. He produced over a hundred kinds of Tibetan
paper products which could sell in markets. His company not only makes the blue
paper, but also produces different sizes and qualities of paper, which can be used at
different social levels. In addition he has refocused research on blue paper, and has
made a plan to promote the quality of papermaking. Formerly, all paper names were
derived from the regions of production such as Kyemshog, Dzamshog, Thoeshog,
Drugshog, Khamshog, Nyishog and Bayu Kaka and so forth. During the period of
the Tibetan local government many paper producers feared taxation of paper and
they did not produce paper openly because they did not want to pay tax to the local
government. This meant many valuable paper making techniques were kept under
cover.
During this period Kyemdong was one of the original places of paper production
and produced the best quality of paper which could be used for Tibetan money,
official documents and writing paper for high Lamas and religious persons more
generally. The paper materials had to last more than the minimum length of time
of a year. If the material from which the paper is made only lasts one year then
the processed paper turns yellow. Therefore, when we are going to make a special
paper, we must ensure that there are no polluted liquids or even a single hair which
could contaminate the quality of the paper. For instance, Tibetan money paper
requires special elements such as Tsigna of wheat, Gara (sugar) and Lalpa (Note:



this is the yogurt which looks like the white stuff in the brain).
In conclusion, all papermaking techniques have regional characteristics, and
usage of these papers has different functions and features. This is just a brief
introduction to the history of paper making and what has been done to protect this
technique in the 1980s.
Nowadays, the Jatson Chumig Handicraft Workshop in Lhasa has protected
several forms of the national cultural heritage among the treasures of the People's
Republic of China and produced a variety of paper items from materials such as
animal skin, bark, silk and satins. The center has also produced over a hundred
artifacts such as modern pictures which look like traditional Thangka art,
envelopes, notebooks, greeting cards, bookmarks, business cards, boxes, book
covers and stationery sets and paper for making exquisite gift-wrappers. Today, it is
widely used for artistic items, invitations, stationery, etc. Due to its unique features
our handmade paper is highly appreciated and recommended by those who collect
it and buy it from the domestic and international markets. We have also received
many awards from the State Council, The Bureau of State Department of Culture,
The Government of the Tibet Autonomous Region and The Bureau of Scientific
and Technological Institution of the Tibet Autonomous Region. Specifically this
project is listed in the 1st group of Tibetan intangible cultural heritage for national
protection. It has also received the certificate at the state level for the invention
of Tibetan paper making. Moreover its paper production is distributed to several
institutions including Tibet University, The Art Gallery of Gedun Chophel, Qinghai
Museum, The Center of Tibetology in Beijing and Beijing National Museum.
Tibetan paper making is listed as an intangible cultural heritage for national
protection. The photos below show products made from Tibetan papers that have
reached the international markets. The main objective of our company is to preserve
the traditional papermaking techniques of the rural and urban areas of Tibet. The
aim of the introduction of our program is to promote this technique and to protect
this priceless cultural heritage. which can now be handed down from generation
to generation. We also wish to distribute these paper products to the international
markets. Any suggestions are welcome and they will be valued dearly.

《བོད་ཤོག་གི་བཟོ་རྩལ་མྱུར་སྐྱོབ་དང་གོང་འཕེལ་བཏང་བའི་བརྒྱུད་རིམ་》ཞེས་པའི་དེབ་

སོགས་པའི་ཕྱག་ལས་འབོར་ཆེན་གནང་ཡོད་པའི་སྐོར་དང་། ཁྲོམ་རར་མཁོ་འདོན་བྱེད་བཞིན་

后记
本书是用藏、汉、英文三种文字,图文并貌的形式详细介
绍了藏纸的生产历史、工艺流程、藏纸的种类、藏纸的用途、
特点等情况。书中特别介绍了在这个珍贵的民族手工艺濒临
失传之际,强巴尊珠先生克服种种困难,先后多次考察不同
的原藏纸产地、寻访民间老艺人、了解藏纸原材料的资源分
布情况、组织人员和培训人才、开发新产品等方面的情况。
在策划和编写这本书的过程中得到了拉萨市城关区政府
的赤来多吉先生为主的有关领导的大力支持,另外,也得到
了西藏自治区发改委和西藏自治区财政厅等单位的支持。还
有发改委的领导次仁旺久先生和有关人士无私提供了本书编
写所需的很多资料。有了以上单位和领导的无私帮助才得以
完成了本书编写的工作。因此,特别感谢相关单位和领导的
支持。
由于本人的水平有限,在编写过程中一定存在很多不足
之处请广大读者指正为盼。
